# Osteóplus — Recruiter Outreach Toolkit

> Templates for sharing the published case study with recruiters, design managers, and senior designers in health tech, wellness, and sports/fitness. Each is tuned to its channel and length budget. Personalize before every send.

---

## 1. Strategy (read once, internalize)

**What outreach actually does.** It earns the recruiter's 60-second skim of the case study. The message itself doesn't get you hired — the case study does. The message has one job: **make them click**.

**Three principles.**

- **Lead with the work, not the ask.** Recruiters reply to *"I made this interesting thing, would you take a look?"* — not *"I'm looking for a job."*
- **Specific over generic.** A message that names their product, a recent talk, or a specific design decision they made beats a templated message every time. If you can't personalize at least one sentence, don't send it.
- **Honest framing wins.** Being a first-portfolio designer with one strong case study is a real position. Pretending to be senior is worse than owning being early-career and showing why your judgment is sound.

**Don't.**

- Send identical messages to 10 people in the same hour.
- Follow up more than twice (7 days + 14 days; then stop).
- Lead with "I'm passionate about UX." Lead with what you made.
- Hide AI assistance. Recruiters in 2026 are AI-aware; concealment damages trust more than disclosure.
- Promise availability you don't have.

**Personalization moves (do before every send).**

- Look at their 3 most recent LinkedIn posts. Reference one.
- Check what they hire for currently. Reference the role only if relevant.
- Find one specific thing about their company — a launched product, an accessibility commitment, a design system post — that connects to your work.

## 2. LinkedIn connection request note (300 character cap)

## 3. LinkedIn DM after they connect (~120 words)

Wait 24–48 hours after they accept, then:

> Hi [Name] — thanks for connecting.
>
> I wanted to share the case study I mentioned: a PWA-first clinic product for adults 35–85 (optimized for users 60+) in Barcelona, covering osteopathy, physiotherapy, and rehab. The piece I'm proudest of is the strategic shift from a medical repository model to an action dashboard — surfacing the next meaningful action instead of asking a senior patient to navigate a passive archive.
>
> It's my first portfolio project, so I'm transparent about evidence gaps and what I'd validate next. Would you have 15 minutes to give me one piece of feedback?
>
> Case study: [your-domain]/case-studies/osteoplus
>
> Either way, thanks for reading.
>
> — Edgar

**Note.** "One piece of feedback" is concrete, low-friction, and treats them as expert. Use "5 minutes" instead of "15 minutes" if they're very senior or you know they're swamped.

## 6. Public LinkedIn post announcing the case study (~180 words)

Posted from your own profile. Goal: visibility + inbound interest. Use sparingly — one post per major case study, not weekly.

> I just shipped my first UI/UX case study: Osteóplus — a PWA-first clinic product for adults 60+ in Barcelona.
>
> The decision I'm most proud of: shifting the product from a medical repository model to an action dashboard.
>
> The repository asked a senior patient to *browse, remember, interpret, and decide.* The action dashboard surfaces the next meaningful action — book, attend, start today's exercise.
>
> For a patient who forgets exercises between visits, that ordering matters more than any feature inventory.
>
> Inside the case study:
>
> → Strategic IA shift, with rejected alternatives and trade-offs
> → WCAG 2.2 AA at the token level (48/56px targets, focus rings, reduced motion)
> → Clinically safe gamification — no punitive streak loss, pain interrupt modal
> → Bilingual ES-first / EN-secondary, full copy deck
> → Evidence discipline — documented vs. expected vs. recommended labels throughout
>
> It's a concept-stage project, transparent about validation gaps. First portfolio — but it's the work I want to be judged on.
>
> Would love feedback from anyone in health tech, accessibility, or senior-friendly design.
>
> [case study link]

**Notes.**
- LinkedIn's "see more" button cuts mobile previews at ~210 characters. Your first 2 lines (the hook + the proud-of decision) are the most important.
- Don't load it with hashtags. 3 max if any: `#UX #Accessibility #Healthcare`.
- Post Tuesday/Wednesday/Thursday morning in your target timezone for best reach.

## 7. Follow-ups after no response

### After 7 days — one nudge (~50 words)

> Hi [Name] — circling back on my note from last week. Adding the case study link in case it got buried: [your-domain]/case-studies/osteoplus.
>
> If now isn't a good moment, no worries — I'll stay out of your inbox. Just wanted to make sure it didn't get lost.
>
> — Edgar

### After 14 days — final nudge (~40 words)

> Hi [Name] — final note from me. If outreach like this isn't useful, I'll stop. If it is, my case study is here: [your-domain]/case-studies/osteoplus. Thanks for your time either way.
>
> — Edgar

**After 14 days with no response, move on.** Don't send a third message. Re-message after 6 months only with substantively new work.

## 8. If they respond positively

Goal: move from message to call without friction.

> Hi [Name] — thanks so much for reading.
>
> I'd love 15 minutes whenever works for you. I've blocked [3 specific times in their timezone, this week and next]. Or if those don't work, here's my calendar: [scheduling link].
>
> Whatever you'd prefer.
>
> — Edgar

**Why specific times.** "Let me know when works" forces them to think. "Here are 3 options" lets them pick one. Specific beats open-ended every time.

---

## 9. If they respond with critique

Recruiters and design managers who give you feedback are giving you a gift. Receive it that way.

> Hi [Name] —
>
> Thanks, this is really useful. The point about [the specific feedback] — I hadn't considered it from that angle, and you're right that [acknowledge the substance].
>
> I'll [specific action — revise this section, validate with users 60+, interview a real Carmen-archetype patient]. I'll send you the updated version if you'd like.
>
> Really grateful for the time.
>
> — Edgar

**Don't.**

- Defend your decisions. Even if you disagree, listen first.
- Promise revisions you won't actually do.
- Ask for a job in the same reply where they gave you feedback.

Keep a simple spreadsheet:

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

**Status values:** `Sent` · `Read` · `Replied` · `Call booked` · `Declined` · `No response (7d)` · `No response (14d)` · `Moved on`.

Don't message the same person again within 6 months unless you have new work.

## 11. Who to reach out to (target tiers)

| Tier | Who | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **1 — Best fit** | Health tech with senior focus / accessibility commitment. Telehealth, chronic care, senior care, rehab platforms. Look for **design managers, principal designers, head of design**. | Your case study is literally about their domain. |
| **2 — Strong fit** | Wellness, fitness, rehab. Even without a senior focus, the gamification-ethics + adherence framing transfers. | Your trade-off thinking on motivation-without-pressure is directly relevant. |
| **3 — Good fit** | Senior designers at companies you admire, even outside health tech, who post about accessibility, design systems, or ethical design. | They'll resonate with the WCAG / evidence discipline / no-shame-copy framing. |
| **Skip** | Generic recruiter accounts that mass-message LinkedIn. | Your case study isn't for them. |

**Recommended starting volume.** 15–20 recipients across the three tiers. Send 3–5 per week, not all at once. Volume matters less than personalization.

## 12. Honest framing reminders

These are not weaknesses — they're context that prevents a recruiter from feeling misled later.

- **You're applying for first roles.** Be transparent. Companies that don't hire juniors will pass — that's fine. Companies that do will value the honesty.
- **The case study is concept-stage.** Don't imply it shipped. The measurement-strategy framing in §13 of the case study handles this — point to it if asked.
- **AI was used for research collection and initial drafts.** If asked directly in an interview, be specific:
  > *"I used Claude to help synthesize research themes from documentation. The strategic decisions — the IA pivot, the no-streak-loss call, the 56px primary CTAs, the design system token architecture — were mine."*
  That answer is hireable. Vagueness is not.
- **You're based in Costa Rica.** Disclose timezone and remote-only / willing-to-relocate up front in the message itself. Don't waste anyone's time with mismatched logistics:
  > *"Based in Costa Rica (UTC−6), available for remote roles or willing to relocate."*

## 13. Pre-send checklist

Walk this before every send.

- [ ] Recipient name is correct and spelled correctly.
- [ ] At least one sentence is personalized to them or their company.
- [ ] The case study URL is the live URL, not a draft / preview.
- [ ] Your LinkedIn and portfolio URLs in the signature are correct.
- [ ] Timezone + remote/relocate clarity is in the message if relevant.
- [ ] Subject line (if email) doesn't oversell.
- [ ] No more than one ask.
- [ ] No "I'm passionate about UX" type phrases.
- [ ] You'd be comfortable if this message got screenshot and shared.
- [ ] You've logged it in the tracker.
